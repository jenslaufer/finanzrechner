Calculate Your Hourly Wage: What You Really Earn

3 April 2026 · 7 min read

Your annual salary is in the contract. But what do you actually earn per hour? Knowing your hourly rate lets you compare job offers, negotiate raises, and evaluate part-time scenarios realistically. Our hourly wage calculator gives you the answer instantly.

The Formula: Annual Salary to Hourly Wage

The basic formula is straightforward:

Hourly Wage = Annual Gross Salary / (Weekly Hours × 52)

52 represents the number of weeks in a year. At 40 hours per week, you work 2,080 hours per year. At 30 hours it is 1,560, and at 20 hours it is 1,040.

Worked Example: Full-Time at €52,000

An annual salary of €52,000 gross at 40 hours per week:

52,000 / (40 × 52) = 52,000 / 2,080 = €25.00 per hour

The monthly salary is €4,333, and the daily rate is €200 (based on 260 working days per year).

Worked Example: Part-Time at 30 Hours

Same annual salary, but only 30 hours per week? The hourly rate goes up:

52,000 / (30 × 52) = 52,000 / 1,560 = €33.33 per hour

In practice, reducing to 30 hours usually means a proportional pay cut. But this shows why the hourly rate is the fairer comparison metric.

Reverse: Hourly Wage to Annual Salary

Freelancers and contractors often calculate the other way. The formula:

Annual Salary = Hourly Rate × Weekly Hours × 52

At €35 per hour and 40 hours per week:

35 × 40 × 52 = €72,800 gross per year

Why Your Hourly Rate Matters

  • Compare job offers: A higher salary with more hours can mean a lower hourly rate.
  • Salary negotiation: Knowing your hourly rate gives you a precise argument.
  • Plan part-time: How does your earnings change with fewer hours?
  • Freelancer pricing: From desired annual income to hourly rate.
  • Value overtime: What is your extra time actually worth?

With proportional pay, the hourly rate stays the same. When the ratio differs, the hourly rate changes — and that is exactly when the calculator becomes valuable.

Gross vs. Net: What You Actually Keep

The calculated hourly rate is gross. After taxes and social contributions, significantly less remains. For a detailed net calculation, use our gross-net calculator or the salary calculator.

Common Mistakes

  • Subtracting vacation days: The 52-week formula already accounts for paid vacation — your salary continues during holidays.
  • Ignoring overtime: Regular unpaid overtime lowers your actual hourly rate.
  • Mixing gross and net: The hourly wage derived from annual salary is always gross.
