Your German payslip arrives and the numbers look confusing. Gross salary at the top, net pay at the bottom — and a column of deductions in between that can easily exceed 40% of what you earn. Understanding what gets deducted, why, and how much is the first step to managing your finances in Germany. Gross vs. Net Salary in Germany

Your gross salary (Bruttogehalt) is the amount your employer agrees to pay you. Your net pay (Nettolohn) is what lands in your bank account after all statutory deductions. The gap between the two depends on four variables: your tax class, your income level, whether you pay church tax, and which federal state you live in.

German employers split the cost of social security contributions with employees. You see only your half on your payslip. Your employer pays an equal amount on top — a cost that never appears on your payslip but directly affects what employers can offer you as gross salary.

Rule of Thumb

A single person in tax class 1 with an average gross salary of €4,000/month takes home roughly 63–67% as net pay. The exact figure depends on income level, state, and church tax status.

Tax Classes (Steuerklassen) 1–6 Explained

Germany assigns every employee to a tax class. Your tax class determines how much income tax is withheld from your payslip each month. Choosing the right combination — especially for married couples — can significantly affect your monthly net pay.

Tax Class Who Qualifies Net Pay Effect
Class 1 Single, divorced, widowed (after year of death), separated Standard withholding — baseline
Class 2 Single parents raising a child in their household Higher than class 1 — relief allowance (Entlastungsbetrag) applies
Class 3 Married / civil partners: the higher earner applies for class 3 Lowest withholding — favourable for the main earner
Class 4 Married / civil partners: default when both earn similarly Same as class 1 — symmetric treatment
Class 5 Married / civil partners: the lower earner paired with class 3 spouse Highest withholding — significant reduction in net pay
Class 6 Second job or additional employment relationship No allowances — maximum withholding applies

Married couples in the 3/5 combination pay the same total annual tax as two class 4 earners — but they receive different monthly net pay amounts. Class 4 with the Faktor-Methode (factor method) distributes the tax burden more evenly between spouses throughout the year.

Social Security Contributions 2026

Four compulsory social insurance branches take a combined employee share of roughly 20% of gross salary, up to each branch's contribution ceiling. Above the ceiling, contributions are capped — meaning high earners pay a lower effective rate.

Branch Employee Share Employer Share Contribution Ceiling (West/month)
Health Insurance (Krankenversicherung) ~7.5–8.5%* ~7.5–8.5%* €5,512.50
Pension Insurance (Rentenversicherung) 9.3% 9.3% €8,050
Unemployment Insurance (Arbeitslosenversicherung) 1.3% 1.3% €8,050
Long-Term Care Insurance (Pflegeversicherung) 1.7–2.6%** 1.7% €5,512.50

* Health insurance rate varies by insurer. The statutory base rate is 14.6% (split equally), plus an insurer-specific additional contribution averaging around 1.7% in 2026 — also split equally.

** Care insurance adds a childless surcharge of +0.6% for employees without children aged 23 and older. In Saxony, employers pay a lower share and employees compensate.

Contribution Ceilings Matter

Pension and unemployment contributions are capped at €8,050/month gross (West Germany 2026). If you earn more, the excess is not subject to these contributions — your effective contribution rate falls above this threshold.

Income Tax: Progressive German Tax Brackets 2026

Germany uses a continuously progressive income tax — not a stepped bracket system. The marginal rate rises smoothly from 0% through to 45% at the top. The annual figures below apply to the taxable income after deductions and allowances.

Taxable Income (annual) Marginal Tax Rate
Up to €12,096 0% (basic allowance — Grundfreibetrag)
€12,097 – €68,429 14% rising progressively to 42%
€68,430 – €277,825 42% (flat — Spitzensteuersatz)
Above €277,826 45% (Reichensteuersatz)

The effective (average) tax rate is always lower than the marginal rate, because only the income within each zone is taxed at that zone's rate. A person with €60,000 taxable income does not pay 42% on all €60,000 — the first €12,096 is tax-free, and rates only reach 42% at the top slice of income.

Several allowances reduce taxable income before these rates apply. The most important for employees are the employee lump-sum deduction (Arbeitnehmer-Pauschbetrag) of €1,230/year and the special expenses deduction (Sonderausgaben-Pauschbetrag) of €36/year.

Solidarity Surcharge and Church Tax

Solidarity Surcharge (Solidaritätszuschlag)

Since 2021, the solidarity surcharge has been abolished for the vast majority of taxpayers. It only applies to individuals with annual income tax above approximately €18,130 (2026 threshold). At that point, a 5.5% surcharge on the income tax liability kicks in — but only on the amount above the threshold. Fewer than 10% of taxpayers are affected.

Church Tax (Kirchensteuer)

Registered members of recognised churches pay church tax, collected by the employer via the payroll. The rate is 8% of income tax in Bavaria and Baden-Württemberg, and 9% in all other federal states. Leaving the church (Kirchenaustritt) removes this deduction — a formal process at the local registry office.

Worked Example: €4,000 Gross, Tax Class 1, No Church Tax, Bavaria

Let's walk through a real calculation. Employee profile: single, no children, tax class 1, statutory health insurer, no church tax, working in Bavaria.

Item Amount (monthly)
Gross salary €4,000.00
Health insurance (employee, ~8.3%) −€332.00
Pension insurance (9.3%) −€372.00
Unemployment insurance (1.3%) −€52.00
Care insurance (1.7% + 0.6% childless surcharge) −€92.00
Total social security (employee) −€848.00
Income tax (approx., class 1) −€571.00
Solidarity surcharge €0.00
Church tax €0.00
Net pay ≈ €2,581.00

Net Pay Formula

Net Pay = Gross − Social Security Contributions − Income Tax − Solidarity Surcharge − Church Tax

Income tax is calculated on taxable income, which equals gross salary minus social security contributions minus employee lump-sum deduction (€1,230/year = €102.50/month).

In this example, the employee retains €2,581 — roughly 64.5% of gross. The employer pays an additional ~€848 in social security on top of the gross salary, making the total employment cost around €4,848 for a €4,000 gross contract.

Tips to Maximise Your Net Salary

1. Choose the Right Tax Class Combination

Married couples have the biggest lever. The 3/5 combination gives the higher earner a significantly higher monthly net pay compared to the default 4/4 split. The annual tax bill stays the same, but cash flow improves for the higher earner throughout the year.

2. Use Your Annual Tax Return

The monthly withholding is often higher than your final tax liability. Claiming all allowable deductions — home office, commuting costs (Pendlerpauschale), work equipment, further education — typically generates a refund. The average German tax refund exceeds €1,000.

3. Switch to a Cheaper Health Insurer

Statutory health insurers (gesetzliche Krankenversicherung) charge different additional contribution rates — ranging from around 1.2% to over 2.5% in 2026. Switching to a cheaper insurer is free and can save €30–70 per month on your German payslip deductions.

4. Maximise Tax-Advantaged Benefits

Employer contributions to company pensions (betriebliche Altersvorsorge) up to 4% of the pension ceiling are free of social security and income tax. A €200/month employer pension contribution costs significantly less than €200 in gross salary and provides the same or better retirement benefit.

5. Claim the Home Office Lump Sum

Germany allows a flat deduction of €6 per home office day, up to €1,260/year. If you work from home regularly, claim this in your tax return — it reduces taxable income directly.

6. Consider Private Health Insurance at Higher Incomes

Employees earning above the compulsory insurance threshold (€73,800 gross/year in 2026) can switch to private health insurance. Depending on age, health, and family situation, premiums can be significantly lower than statutory contributions — raising net pay noticeably.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between gross and net salary in Germany?

Gross salary is your total contractual pay before any deductions. Net pay is the amount credited to your bank account after income tax, solidarity surcharge, church tax (if applicable), and your half of social security contributions are deducted.

How much do I take home from €3,000 gross?

A single person in tax class 1 earning €3,000 gross takes home approximately €1,970–€2,050/month depending on health insurer and state. Use the gross net salary calculator for a precise figure.

Does the federal state affect net pay?

Directly, only through care insurance (Saxony has different employer/employee split) and church tax rates (8% in Bavaria and Baden-Württemberg vs. 9% elsewhere). Income tax and other social security rates are uniform across Germany.

Are bonus payments taxed differently?

No — bonuses are added to your regular monthly salary and taxed at your marginal rate for that month. This can temporarily push you into a higher withholding bracket. Your annual tax return levels this out, often generating a refund if the bonus inflated one month's withholding artificially.

Conclusion

Your German payslip deducts income tax, solidarity surcharge, church tax, and four social security contributions. The combined effect reduces a €4,000 gross salary to roughly €2,580 net — a deduction of about 35%. Understanding each line item lets you identify where the money goes and which levers you can use to keep more of your gross salary as net pay.

  1. Tax class matters most for married couples — class 3/5 changes monthly cash flow significantly.
  2. File your tax return every year — most employees get money back.
  3. Compare health insurers — the additional contribution rate varies by over 1% between insurers.
  4. Use employer benefits — company pension contributions and tax-free allowances reduce gross-to-net loss.
  5. Use the calculator — every situation is different. Run your own numbers.

The difference between gross and net is not a fixed percentage — it depends on your tax class, income, health insurer, state, and church membership. Know your numbers.
