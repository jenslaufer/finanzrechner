Loan Calculator: How to Calculate Your Monthly Payment

March 29, 2026 · 8 min read

Calculating a loan payment sounds complicated — but it isn't. Whether it's a car loan, personal loan, or mortgage: your monthly payment depends on three factors — loan amount, interest rate, and term. Know these three numbers and you can figure out your payment in seconds. Our loan calculator does exactly that.

How Does a Loan Calculator Work?

A loan calculator takes your inputs — loan amount, interest rate, and term — and computes the monthly payment using the amortization formula. It also shows you the total interest paid and how your loan balance decreases over time.

The three input values determine everything:

  • Loan amount: The principal you borrow. The higher it is, the higher your monthly payment — given the same term and interest rate.
  • Interest rate (APR): The annual cost of borrowing. The APR includes all fees and is the only meaningful comparison metric between loan offers.
  • Term: In months or years. A longer term lowers your monthly payment but significantly increases total loan interest paid.

The Amortization Formula Explained

Most installment loans use amortization. This means you pay the same fixed amount every month. Within that fixed payment, the ratio of interest to principal shifts over time.

At the beginning, the interest portion is high because the remaining balance is large. With each payment, the balance shrinks, interest decreases, and more of your payment goes toward principal. By the end, your payment is almost entirely principal.

The mathematical formula for the monthly payment is:

Amortization Formula

Payment = P × (r × (1 + r)^n) / ((1 + r)^n − 1)

P = principal (loan amount), r = monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12), n = number of monthly payments

An example: You borrow $20,000 at 5% APR for 5 years.

  • r = 0.05 ÷ 12 = 0.004167
  • n = 60 months
  • Payment = 20,000 × (0.004167 × 1.004167^60) / (1.004167^60 − 1) = $377.42

Total repayment: 60 × $377.42 = $22,645. The loan interest costs $2,645 — about 13% of the principal.

Interest Rate Comparison: Finding the Best Deal

The interest rate is the single biggest lever on loan cost. Even half a percentage point difference saves thousands on larger loans. The following table shows the effect of different rates on a $20,000 loan over 5 years:

Interest Rate (APR) Monthly Payment Total Interest Total Cost
3.0% $359.37 $1,562 $21,562
5.0% $377.42 $2,645 $22,645
7.0% $396.02 $3,761 $23,761
9.0% $415.17 $4,910 $24,910

Between 3% and 9% lies a $3,350 difference in total interest. On larger loans or longer terms, the gap grows even wider. Always compare multiple offers before signing.

Term Length vs. Monthly Payment

A longer term makes the monthly payment more comfortable — but you pay more interest overall. Here's a $30,000 loan at 5% to illustrate:

Term Monthly Payment Total Interest Total Cost
3 years $899.13 $2,369 $32,369
5 years $566.14 $3,968 $33,968
7 years $424.26 $5,638 $35,638
10 years $318.20 $8,184 $38,184

Between 3 and 10 years, interest costs triple — from $2,369 to $8,184. The rule of thumb: choose the shortest term you can afford without straining your monthly budget.

Extra Payments: The Biggest Lever

Extra payments are additional amounts paid toward the principal — on top of your regular payment. They work like a turbo because every extra dollar reduces the remaining balance immediately. After that, less interest accrues, and the loan is paid off faster.

An example: $50,000 loan, 4% interest, 10-year term. Without extra payments, you pay about $10,748 in total interest. With $2,000 in annual extra payments, you save roughly $2,500 in interest and are debt-free about 2 years early.

Check Your Loan Terms

Not every loan allows penalty-free extra payments. Check your loan agreement for prepayment clauses before making additional payments. Some lenders charge a prepayment penalty, while others allow unlimited extra payments. For mortgages, prepayment options vary widely — negotiate this before signing.

Fixed-Rate Period: What It Means for Mortgages

For mortgages, the interest rate isn't locked in for the entire loan term — only for a fixed-rate period. Common periods are 5, 10, 15, or 30 years.

After the fixed-rate period expires, the remaining balance must be refinanced at current market rates. If rates have risen, the new terms will be more expensive.

Short vs. Long Fixed-Rate Period

  • Short period (5–10 years): Lower initial rate, but higher risk if rates rise at refinancing time.
  • Long period (15–30 years): Higher initial rate, but certainty. You know exactly what you'll pay.

In low-rate environments, locking in a longer fixed period makes sense — you secure cheap financing. When rates are high, a shorter period may work if you expect rates to fall.

Remaining Balance: What's Left After the Fixed Period

The remaining balance is the amount still owed when the fixed-rate period ends. It depends on three factors: loan amount, repayment rate, and fixed-rate duration.

A typical mortgage example:

Parameter Value
Loan amount $300,000
Interest rate 3.5%
Initial repayment rate 2%
Monthly payment $1,375
Balance after 10 years ~$228,000
Balance after 15 years ~$186,000

After 10 years, 76% of the original loan is still outstanding. Two levers reduce the remaining balance: a higher repayment rate or extra payments. Increasing the repayment rate from 2% to 3% drops the 10-year balance to about $199,000.

Common Mistakes When Taking Out a Loan

1. Focusing only on the monthly payment

A low payment sounds appealing, but often comes from a long term. The result: thousands more in interest. Always compare total cost, not just the monthly figure.

2. Comparing nominal rates instead of APR

The nominal rate excludes fees. The APR includes everything — it's the only metric that allows a fair comparison between offers.

3. Not building in a buffer

Your payment should be no more than 35–40% of your net income. Planning on the edge risks missed payments when unexpected expenses hit — car repairs, medical bills, job changes.

4. Accepting payment protection insurance without review

Lenders often offer payment protection insurance alongside the loan. In most cases, it's overpriced and unnecessary. An existing life insurance or disability policy usually provides better and cheaper coverage.

5. Ignoring the prepayment option

Failing to secure prepayment rights in your contract means giving up one of the most effective tools for saving on loan interest. Especially for mortgages, this option should be non-negotiable.

When to Use a Loan Calculator

A loan calculator is invaluable in several situations:

  • Before meeting your lender: Know which payment fits your budget and which term makes sense. Negotiate from a position of knowledge.
  • When comparing offers: Run different interest rates and terms side by side. Spot the cheapest loan instantly.
  • For refinancing decisions: Is it worth replacing your current loan with a cheaper one? The calculator shows you.
  • For mortgage planning: Calculate the remaining balance after the fixed period, simulate extra payments, compare different repayment rates.

Conclusion

Calculating a loan payment is not rocket science. The key takeaways:

  1. Three variables control everything: loan amount, interest rate, term.
  2. Always compare APR — not the nominal rate.
  3. Choose the shortest affordable term — every year less saves hundreds to thousands in interest.
  4. Use extra payments — the most effective way to reduce loan interest costs.
  5. Watch the remaining balance — especially for mortgages with fixed-rate periods.

A good loan isn't the one with the lowest payment — it's the one with the lowest total cost.
